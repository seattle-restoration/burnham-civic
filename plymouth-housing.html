Plymouth Housing

$69.5 million in revenue, a $22.4 million deficit, and a Bellevue facility that went from 5 police calls to 148 in one year.

Overview

Plymouth Housing is one of the largest permanent supportive housing providers in King County. Founded in 1980, it operates 18 buildings housing over 2,200 residents. It is also the largest veteran housing provider in the Pacific Northwest, with roughly one in seven residents being a veteran. Plymouth operates on a Housing First model, meaning residents are not required to demonstrate sobriety or treatment compliance to receive housing.

Leadership

CEO Karen Lee joined in 2022 from Pioneer Human Services. She is paid $398,787 per year. COO Andrea Carnes earns $278,863. CFO John Siddall earns $262,637. Total salary and wage expense across the organization was $29.6 million in 2024, representing 32.2% of total expenses.

The board includes representatives from Amazon, Zillow, Google, Deloitte, Perkins Coie, and WaFd Bank. Board chair is Sarah Meyer of the Kaphan Foundation. Board treasurer is Mike Schlect of Deloitte.

The 2019 revenue spike ($75.3M against only $36.1M in expenses) suggests a major capital campaign or large one-time grant. Plymouth has been spending down those reserves at an accelerating rate. At the current burn rate, the surplus will be gone within a few years.

The Bellevue Problem

Plymouth Crossing, a facility in Bellevue, generated 5 police calls in late 2023. In 2024, that number was 148. That is not a gradual increase. That is a facility that lost control.

Reports from the facility describe rampant drug use, assaults, feces in hallways, and bed bug infestations. Proposals for similar Plymouth facilities in Kenmore and Redmond have met significant community pushback over crime and public safety impacts.

The Belltown Attack

A Plymouth Housing resident with a violent criminal history allegedly attacked a woman with a baseball bat in Belltown, causing injuries that required skull surgery. The resident was housed by Plymouth under the Housing First model, which does not screen for criminal history or require behavioral compliance.

Harm Reduction Policy

Plymouth explicitly allows drug consumption within units under a "harm reduction" philosophy. Their website states they help residents "minimize harms" of substance use. Critics argue this policy enables continued drug use rather than promoting recovery, and that the organization measures success by retention rate (93% of residents stay housed) rather than by whether residents are actually getting better.

Staying in the building is the metric. Recovery is not.

KCRHA Relationship

Plymouth is a major contracted provider through KCRHA's Coordinated Entry for All system. Housing units are filled through the Housing Triage Tool administered by KCRHA. The $51.9 million in "contributions" listed on Plymouth's 990 is largely government funding routed through KCRHA, the City of Seattle, King County, and HUD Continuum of Care grants.

Key Questions

1. How does Plymouth justify a CEO salary of nearly $400,000 while running a $22.4 million annual deficit?

2. What specific outcomes does Plymouth track beyond retention rate? What percentage of residents achieve employment, sobriety, or independent living?

3. What operational changes were made at Plymouth Crossing after police calls increased from 5 to 148?

4. How much of Plymouth's government funding comes through KCRHA contracts versus direct city, county, and federal grants?

5. At the current burn rate, when does the reserve fund run out?
