Pierce County Petition for Prosecutorial Accountability

A framework for grand jury petitions and sheriff-led accountability coalitions in Washington State

Published March 21, 2026 | Burnham Civic

The Problem

In Pierce County and across Washington State, elected sheriffs face a structural imbalance: they hold constitutional authority to arrest and keep the peace, but the charging decision belongs exclusively to the county prosecutor. When the prosecutor's office becomes adversarial toward the sheriff, the entire justice system breaks down. Cases get declined. Power plays replace professional courtesy. The public loses.

This is not a partisan issue. It is a structural one. The Founders designed separated powers precisely to prevent any single office from consolidating control over the justice system. When that separation erodes, citizens must act.

Legal Authority: What a Sheriff Can Do

Constitutional Foundation

The Office of Sheriff is established in the Washington State Constitution, Article XI, Section 5. The sheriff is the chief executive officer and conservator of the peace of the county. The modern sheriff retains historic common law powers unless changed by statute.

Key statutes:

  • RCW 36.28.010 : Sheriff as conservator of the peace
  • RCW 36.28.011 : Duty to enforce all criminal laws
  • Art. XI, Sec. 5 : Constitutional protection of the office

Grand Jury Authority

Under RCW 10.27, a grand jury of 12 citizens can be impaneled by the superior court to investigate criminal activity and corruption. The grand jury can issue reports on matters of broad public policy.

The gap: RCW 10.27.030 requires a "public attorney" to petition the court for a grand jury. Normally this is the county prosecutor. When the prosecutor IS the subject of investigation, the following alternatives exist:

  • Attorney General can petition for a grand jury in any county
  • Special prosecutor can be appointed by the court where a conflict of interest exists
  • Sheriff can formally request AG investigation, documenting the conflict

The Five-Phase Framework

Phase 1: Committee of Correspondence (Immediate)

Document and communicate

  • Weekly public report from Sheriff's Office documenting every interaction with the prosecutor's office
  • Every declined charge catalogued with case details
  • Every protocol breach (unilateral calendar scheduling, Loudermill power plays, etc.) logged
  • Public communication via social media, local media, and direct constituent outreach
Phase 2: Formal Petition (30 Days)

Exhaust every legal remedy on the record

  • Formal written request to the Attorney General to investigate the prosecutor's office for abuse of authority
  • Formal request to Pierce County Superior Court for appointment of a special prosecutor under conflict of interest provisions
  • Formal request for a grand jury under RCW 10.27.030
  • Each denial becomes part of the public record. The Declaration of Independence was a list of petitions denied.
Phase 3: Committee of Safety (60-90 Days)

Build parallel accountability structures

  • Sheriff's Citizen Review Board: voluntary panel of respected county residents reviewing declined prosecutions
  • Independent charging recommendation reports: publish evidence for every arrest where the prosecutor declines charges
  • Direct relationship with the court: warrants, protection orders, civil process all bypass the prosecutor
Phase 4: Continental Congress (6 Months)

Multi-county sheriff coalition

  • Washington State Sheriffs' Coalition for Prosecutorial Accountability
  • Shared data on declined prosecutions, charging patterns, and prosecutor-sheriff conflicts
  • Model legislation: independent grand jury petition mechanism that does not require a prosecutor's approval
Phase 5: The Declaration (When Ready)

Make the case to the voters

  • Formal published document: "The State of Justice in Pierce County"
  • Every grievance documented. Every petition denied. Every case dropped.
  • Electoral accountability: the prosecutor is elected. The voters decide.

Model Legislation: Independent Grand Jury Petition

Washington State currently requires a "public attorney" (prosecutor or AG) to petition for a grand jury. This creates a structural conflict when the prosecutor is the subject of investigation. Proposed amendment to RCW 10.27.030:

Proposed addition: "An elected county sheriff or a petition signed by not fewer than 500 registered voters of the county may petition the superior court for the impaneling of a grand jury when the county prosecuting attorney has a conflict of interest in the matter to be investigated, as determined by the court."

This preserves judicial oversight (the court still decides whether to impanel) while removing the prosecutor's gatekeeping function in conflict situations.

Historical Precedent

This framework follows the pattern of the American Revolution, adapted for modern constitutional governance:

  • Committees of Correspondence (1772): Parallel communication when official channels are captured
  • Petitions to the Crown (1774): Formal, on-the-record requests through proper channels
  • Committees of Safety (1775): Parallel governance structures when official ones fail
  • Continental Congress (1774-1776): Multi-colony coordination and shared authority
  • Declaration of Independence (1776): Public accounting of grievances after all remedies exhausted

The Founders exhausted every legal remedy before acting. They documented every refusal. They built coalitions. They made their case to the people. This framework does the same.
