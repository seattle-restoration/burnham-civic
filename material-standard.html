The Standard Seattle Forgot

How Seattle went from brick and terracotta to glass and vinyl siding, and what a material standard would change.

Walk through Pioneer Square and you are walking past buildings made of brick, terracotta, and stone. Buildings that were built after the Great Fire of 1889, when the city decided that wood was not good enough and passed an ordinance requiring masonry construction in the commercial district. Those buildings are still standing 137 years later. They are still beautiful. They are still the most valuable real estate in the neighborhood.

Now walk through South Lake Union. Glass. Vinyl siding. Metal panel. Buildings that looked dated the day they opened and will need major facade repairs within 20 years. Nobody required these materials. Nobody prevented better ones. The standard simply disappeared, and for 30 years nobody noticed.

What We Lost

Seattle has a long history of building with quality materials. The Smith Tower (1914) used terracotta and marble. The Arctic Building (1917) is clad in terracotta walruses. The Rainier Club (1904) is brick and stone. King Street Station (1906) was modeled after the Campanile in Venice and built with brick and terra cotta that has lasted over a century.

These buildings were not expensive vanity projects. They were the standard. Builders used durable materials because the city expected it, the market demanded it, and the economics made sense over a 50- to 100-year time horizon.

Somewhere around the 1990s, the standard changed. Developers discovered that glass curtain walls and vinyl rain screen systems were cheaper to install. Permitting did not distinguish between a brick building and a glass box. The design review process focused on massing and setbacks, not materials. And so the city filled up with buildings made of the cheapest materials that code would allow.

What Glass Actually Costs

Glass looks modern. It also performs terribly.

2x the electricity consumption of masonry buildings (heating and cooling)

45% of BC glass high-rises experienced water infiltration failures

20-30 years typical facade lifespan for glass curtain wall systems

100+ years typical facade lifespan for brick, stone, and terracotta

A glass curtain wall building uses roughly twice the energy of a comparable masonry building for heating and cooling. Glass is a terrible insulator. In a city that cares about carbon emissions, this should matter more than it does.

The durability problem is worse. A study of glass high-rises in British Columbia found that 45% experienced water infiltration through their curtain wall systems. Water infiltration leads to mold, structural damage, and expensive repairs. The average glass curtain wall system has a useful life of 20 to 30 years before it needs major repair or replacement. Brick lasts a century. Stone lasts longer.

The upfront cost savings of glass disappear entirely when you account for energy costs, maintenance, and replacement over the building's life. Glass is cheap to build and expensive to own.

Cities That Got It Right

Other cities have not forgotten their material standards.

Minneapolis mandates limestone and brick for buildings in its downtown heritage zones. The city's design guidelines explicitly require durable, regionally appropriate materials for any new construction that faces a public street.

Copenhagen mandates material quality through its municipal plan. New buildings must demonstrate material durability and aesthetic compatibility with their surroundings. The result is a city where new construction enhances rather than degrades the streetscape.

Charleston, South Carolina has maintained material standards for decades through its Board of Architectural Review. Every new building in the historic district must use approved materials. The result: Charleston consistently ranks as one of the most beautiful cities in America, and its property values reflect it.

Santa Fe, New Mexico has the closest analogy to what we are proposing. Santa Fe's Historic Districts Review Board enforces a codified material mandate: adobe and pueblo-style construction is required within the historic zones. It is the only city in America where a specific regional material is required by code, not just encouraged. Property values prove the market rewards it. Santa Fe's material identity is its economic engine.

Alys Beach, Florida is a planned community on the Panhandle where every building must be white masonry. The architectural code is strict and the results are extraordinary: Alys Beach homes sell at a premium over every neighboring town on 30A. A material mandate did not kill development. It created the most valuable real estate on the Gulf Coast.

These are not anti-development cities. They are cities that understood something simple: the quality of what you build determines the quality of where you live. Requiring good materials does not stop development. It makes development better.

The Seattle Material Palette

Seattle already knows what good materials look like. The top 10 buildings by assessed value in the city use durable, high-quality materials. The market rewards quality. The code just does not require it.

Burnham Civic proposes a material standard for Seattle public buildings and publicly subsidized construction. Seven approved materials, chosen for durability, regional appropriateness, and proven performance in the Pacific Northwest climate:

  • Brick (clay masonry, locally sourced where possible)
  • Terracotta (glazed and unglazed, in the Seattle tradition)
  • Natural stone (granite, limestone, sandstone)
  • Mass timber (CLT, glulam, heavy timber)
  • Architectural concrete (board-formed, exposed aggregate, precast)
  • Copper and bronze (cladding, trim, and ornamental)
  • Weathering steel (Cor-Ten, for accent and structural expression)
  • Precast concrete (board-formed, exposed aggregate, Brutalist heritage)

The banned list is equally important:

  • Vinyl siding (in any application on public-facing facades)
  • EIFS (Exterior Insulation and Finish Systems, synthetic stucco)
  • Fiber cement panel (as a primary cladding material)
  • Metal composite panel (aluminum composite, as primary cladding)

Glass is not banned. But the standard would limit glass to no more than 60% of any public-facing facade, consistent with energy performance and durability goals. At least 40% of every street-facing elevation must use approved heritage materials.

The Puget Standard: Trim Team Color Palette

Materials are half the equation. The other half is what the city looks like at street level: the railings, bollards, benches, pole bases, park gates, and public fixtures that define the feel of a neighborhood. Right now Seattle has no unified color system for any of it. Every fixture is a different shade of whatever the last contractor had left over.

The Burnham Civic 47 Shifts operation includes a Trim Team that applies a unified color palette to every public fixture in the city. The palette is called the Puget Standard, drawn from Puget Sound, the evergreens, and Seattle's natural landscape. Not trendy. Timeless.

The Airplane Test

San Francisco passes a test that Seattle fails. When you descend into SFO at sunset, you can see the city's character from the air. The white and pastel Victorians. The terracotta downtown. A skyline that looks like it belongs somewhere specific. You know where you are before the wheels touch down.

Fly into SeaTac and look out the window. Glass. Grey panel. More glass. Nothing tells you this is Seattle and not Charlotte or Phoenix or any other city that let developers build whatever was cheapest. A material standard is not just about what buildings look like at street level. It is about whether a city has a visible identity at all. Seattle should be recognizable from 10,000 feet.

The Charleston Model

Charleston, South Carolina has maintained architectural standards for decades through its Board of Architectural Review. The BAR reviews every exterior alteration in the historic district. It has teeth. It has clear material guidelines. And critically, it has insulated itself from ideological capture. The board is composed of practitioners, not activists. Architects, builders, preservationists. People who know what lasts.

Burnham Civic is engaging the City of Charleston directly to study how the BAR is structured, how members are selected, and how the board has maintained its standards without being overrun by fads or political pressure. The goal is to bring that model to Seattle.

Bring the Kilns Home

There is a supply chain problem hiding inside the material question. Seattle's terra cotta was manufactured locally until 1932. Denny-Renton Clay in Renton made the Arctic Building's walrus heads. Northern Clay Company in Auburn made the Frederick & Nelson building, the Joshua Green Building, and the Securities Building. Gladding McBean operated a plant in Auburn from 1925 to 1932. All three closed during the Depression, and no replacement has emerged in nearly a century.

Today the two principal architectural terra cotta manufacturers in the United States are Boston Valley Terra Cotta in Hamburg, New York, and Gladding McBean in Lincoln, California, the latter in continuous operation since 1875. Every piece of terra cotta used in the Pacific Northwest ships at least 800 miles. Custom glaze work takes 4 to 8 months. Large facade restorations take 18 to 24 months.

A material standard solves this. If Seattle requires heritage materials on street-facing elevations, the volume of terra cotta demand justifies reopening a PNW production facility. The clay deposits in the Renton-Auburn corridor that supported three manufacturers for decades are still there. The raw materials exist. The skilled labor can be trained. What has been missing is guaranteed demand. Policy creates demand. Demand justifies a kiln. A kiln drops cost and lead time from months to weeks. Seattle buildings get built with Seattle clay.

Where the Materials Come From

The objection to a real architectural revival is always the same. We cannot get the materials. Nobody makes them anymore. It costs too much. None of that is true. Every material that built old Seattle is still in the ground or still in production. Here is the actual supply chain for building neoclassical and art deco architecture again.

The materials sort into three buckets, and the buckets are the strategy. Some we import because the rock simply is not under us. Some we already make in the region right now. And some we used to make and could make again. Here is each material, what it does, what it costs installed today, and where it comes from.

Limestone is the import, and it is not as expensive as people assume. Dimensional limestone is the smooth ashlar cladding that reads as a stone building. It comes from Indiana, the Bedford and Salem freestone that clad the Empire State Building and the Pentagon, and it runs roughly $45 to $110 per square foot installed for anchored dimensional panels, or $30 to $50 as thin veneer. Washington has no building-grade limestone of its own and never has. The closest deposit is only about 45 miles away in the Cascade foothills near Granite Falls, but it is cement and lime grade, fractured and shot through with chert, and cannot be sawn into facade blocks. The nearest limestone we could actually build with is in Utah, and the nearest dimensional carbonate is Idaho and Montana travertine. So limestone we buy and ship in.

Sandstone is the local stone we already own. Wilkeson sandstone from Pierce County, an hour from downtown, clad the State Capitol dome and was so dense it never needed waterproofing. The quarry is still active. Sandstone runs roughly $40 to $90 per square foot installed as commercial dimensional cladding and less as thin veneer, and the generic warning that sandstone fails in wet climates does not apply to Wilkeson, which has stood in Washington rain for a century. This is our limestone substitute, and it is regional.

Granite and marble are the durable and luxury tiers. Granite runs about $60 to $150 per square foot installed for commercial anchored panels and comes from Cold Spring in Minnesota, the Mount Airy quarry in North Carolina (the largest open-face granite quarry in the world), and our own dormant Index granite from Snohomish County, which went into the Smith Tower. Marble is the top of the range at $50 to $200 and up, from Danby in Vermont (the largest underground marble quarry in the world, supplier to the Jefferson Memorial), Colorado Yule, and Georgia for statuary. Both are imports.

Brick never left, and it is made here. Face-brick veneer is about $11 per square foot as residential siding, which is the single hardest cost figure in this whole picture (it comes straight off an RSMeans cost-engineering study), and roughly $30 to $55 installed as a commercial full-bed wall over structural backup. Either way it is one of the most affordable durable claddings there is. Mutual Materials, headquartered in Bellevue and family-owned since 1900, is the largest masonry manufacturer in the Pacific Northwest and still fires brick across the region.

Terra cotta is the Seattle signature and the one real local gap. Glazed terra cotta rainscreen, the material of the Smith Tower and the Arctic Building walruses, runs about $55 to $120 per square foot installed. The two principal American makers, Boston Valley in New York and Gladding McBean in California, are 800 miles away. But the Renton and Auburn clay that fed three local plants until the 1930s is still in the ground. Guaranteed demand reopens a kiln, which is the whole argument of the section above.

Cast stone and GFRC are the cost unlock. This is the direct answer to the claim that beautiful is too expensive. Glass fiber reinforced concrete panels run about $35 to $55 per square foot installed, roughly half the cost of natural stone and a third of the weight, and they can be molded to read as limestone, sandstone, or terra cotta. Cast stone is priced by the piece rather than the square foot, made to order for the cornices, capitals, balustrades, and window surrounds that give a building its classical detail. The reason people think ornament is unaffordable is that they picture a mason hand-carving every capital out of solid rock. You do not. You model the master once and cast the repeats. The detail that looks expensive is, in practice, a mold. These can be fabricated regionally.

Bronze is the deco accent, priced by the piece. Architectural bronze, the doors, grilles, elevator surrounds, and spandrels of art deco, is a bespoke item from ornamental metal foundries, priced by weight and labor rather than by the square foot. It belongs as accent, not field cladding, and it is sourced nationally.

Lime is the binder, and it is a domestic gap. Lime mortar and lime plaster, the soft breathable binders that real masonry needs instead of hard portland cement, carry a modest material premium and are the correct choice for stone and historic walls. America's premium building lime is largely imported from France today. It is the very product Roche Harbor used to make, which is the second dormant local industry hiding inside the material question.

Put the numbers next to each other and the argument that we cannot afford to build beautifully does not just weaken, it inverts. At real commercial quality, GFRC ($35 to $55), architectural precast ($40 to $80), brick, and even anchored dimensional limestone ($45 to $110) cost the same as or less than the high-performance unitized glass curtain wall that actually clads downtown towers, which runs $90 to $175 a square foot. The cheap glass box is a myth at the tier that gets built: basic stick-built glazing is cheap, but the engineered, insulated, unitized glass on a real tower is among the most expensive skins there is. We are not choosing between cheap glass and expensive stone. We are choosing between a glass facade that fails in 25 years and a stone, brick, or terra cotta facade that lasts a century, often at a lower price.

How the Materials Get Here

There is a transport cost buried in all of this, and getting it right matters, because the intuition most people have is wrong. Seattle is mountain-locked, and the heaviest materials we cannot quarry locally have to cross the Cascades. But heavy is not the same as freight-sensitive. Crushed stone and aggregate are so cheap per ton that freight is 30 to 65 percent of their delivered cost, which is why nobody ships gravel across the country. Dimensional building stone is the opposite. A cubic foot of Indiana limestone block leaves the quarry worth roughly $100, about $1,300 a ton, so the freight to move it, even 2,000 miles, is only somewhere between 3 and 12 percent of its delivered cost. High-value stone can cross a continent economically. That is exactly why the Empire State Building could be clad in Indiana limestone in the first place.

So the honest tunnel math is more interesting than "a tunnel makes limestone cheap." For long-haul rail stone from Indiana or Utah, a flat trans-Cascade bore models out to maybe a 3 to 10 percent cut in delivered cost, because the mountain is only one segment of a long flat haul. Where the tunnel actually pays is two places. First, reliability: the I-90 and Snoqualmie corridor closes around 29 times a winter, and one 89-hour closure was measured at $27.9 million in lost output. For construction on a schedule, killing that tail risk is worth more than the average per-ton price. Second, regional and truck-dependent materials: Idaho travertine, regional stone, and local distribution move by truck at about 15.6 cents per ton-mile, three times the 5 cents of rail. A tunnel that lets that freight shift to rail and stops getting shut down by avalanches models an 8 to 20 percent cut in delivered cost on those materials. These are modeled estimates from public freight rates and closure data, not quoted figures.

This is where the material standard meets the Trump Tunnels proposal. You do not justify a tunnel on the price of stone. You justify it on freight reliability for an entire mountain-locked economy, and cheaper, steadier delivery of heavy building materials is one of the things it quietly hands you. The standard says build with durable materials. The tunnel is part of the supply line that keeps them affordable to get here.

Repricing With Ambition

Every price on this page assumes today's supply chain: buy on the open market, ship it in, and pay each middleman along the way. The civic question is what those numbers become if we change the supply chain itself. There are two levers, owning the supply and lowering the cost of moving it, and it is worth being honest about how much each one actually moves the number, because the obvious answer is not the right one.

Owning a quarry feels like it should slash the cost. It does not, and the reason is instructive. For a stone cladding panel, the quarry block itself is only about 10 to 20 percent of the installed price. The expensive parts are fabrication, distribution margin, freight, and installation labor, and installation labor alone, which no amount of owning the quarry can touch, is 25 to 40 percent. If a civic entity owned the quarry and the fabrication plant, it could strip out the upstream margin, realistically 15 to 30 percent of installed cost, but only if it ran enough sustained volume to keep an expensive plant busy. A greenfield dimensional quarry runs $6 to $15 million and three to five years through permitting, plus another $5 to $15 million for a fabrication plant. Amortized over a single building program, that is worse per square foot than buying from someone who already sells to the whole market.

The smarter version of the Idaho idea is not to dig our own hole. It is to sign an offtake agreement or take an equity stake in operators who already own proven reserves and modern saws: Idaho Travertine, sitting on more than 100 million cubic feet of usable travertine; Yellowstone Rock in Montana, with integrated wire-saw fabrication; and Mountain Valley Stone in Utah, whose Utah White Limestone is the closest thing in the West to the white civic look of Indiana limestone. Fund incremental capacity, lock in a block allocation, and capture most of the removable margin at a fraction of the cost and time of building from scratch.

Lime is a different kind of problem, and the most strategically interesting one. No true natural hydraulic lime, the binder real masonry wants, is made in America at all. It ships from France and Portugal, and the same bag that costs about $19 in Britain costs around $60 here. A tunnel does nothing for that, because it is an ocean haul. The fix is domestic production, which is the deeper meaning of bringing the kilns home. The honest caveat: Washington's limestone is high-calcium, which is excellent for ordinary quicklime and hydrated lime, the kind of lime Roche Harbor actually made, but it is the wrong chemistry for French-style natural hydraulic lime, which needs an impure, clay-rich limestone. So the achievable domestic win is ordinary building lime; true natural hydraulic lime would need the right deposit or an engineered equivalent. Either way, the lime gap is a production gap, not a transport gap.

Put the levers in order of what they return per dollar and the ranking is honest rather than romantic. Cheaper, more reliable transport and offtake agreements come first, because they touch every ton at low capital risk. Regional lime production comes next, because there is a real import-substitution gap to close. A stake in a western stone quarry comes third. And reopening a terra cotta kiln, at $15 to $40 million and against a near-vanished skilled workforce, comes last on cost, even though it may come first on heritage: it would be only the third architectural terra cotta plant in the country, a regional monopoly on the material that defines Seattle's best buildings. Owning the dirt saves less than it feels like it should, because the dirt is the cheap part. The ambition is not to pretend stone is free. It is to attack the real cost drivers, transport reliability, upstream margin, and a domestic lime industry we let die, and to lean on the light, regional materials we already have, Wilkeson sandstone, Mutual Materials brick, mass timber, GFRC, and a reopened terra cotta kiln, for everything they can carry.

The full operational plan, the sourcing, the phasing, the rough capex, and the one assay that gates it, is laid out in Building the Supply.

Grow the Frame, Clad the Identity

The deepest objection to a stone revival is that load-bearing masonry is dead, that nobody builds solid stone anymore. That is true, and it does not matter, because the buildings we admire were never solid stone either. The Smith Tower is a steel frame with a terra cotta and granite skin hung on it. Terra cotta became the signature skyscraper material precisely because it was lightweight fireproof cladding on a frame. A classical facade has been a veneer on a structure for over a century. The frame-and-skin building is the tradition, not a cheat on it.

That opens the move that fits Seattle exactly: build the structure in mass timber and clad it in the classical materials above. Mass timber, glulam columns and cross-laminated timber floors, is the one structural material the Pacific Northwest actually grows. It runs about $30 to $70 per square foot of floor, at or slightly above the cost of steel and concrete on a material basis, but it erects 25 to 30 percent faster, weighs up to 80 percent less, and carries a fraction of the carbon. A Pacific Northwest project, the First Tech Federal Credit Union building, came in about 4 percent under its steel design and finished months early. Modern code allows it up to 18 stories, so this is not limited to small buildings. The light cladding options, terra cotta rainscreen, thin stone, and GFRC, play directly to a timber frame's strength, and a durable masonry skin over a proper weather barrier is one of the best envelopes you can give wood, because it keeps the structure dry for a century. We grow the frame and clad it in our identity. The two halves of the standard were made for each other.

Who Builds It

The real constraint is not material, it is hands. Carvers, ornamental plasterers, terra cotta modelers, bronze casters. America let those trades thin out for two generations, but they did not disappear, and they are being rebuilt on purpose. The American College of the Building Arts in Charleston is the only accredited four-year college in the country granting building-arts degrees, with programs in architectural stone carving, timber framing, plaster, ironwork, and classical design. Seattle can copy that model directly: pair a regional trades school with the maintenance backlog on the city's own historic building stock, and you train the workforce on real work that needs doing anyway.

Technology closes the rest of the gap. CNC stone milling, robotic carving, and 3D-printed molds let a small number of skilled artisans produce ornament at a volume that used to take a guild. Companies like Monumental Labs, a robotic stone-carving shop founded in New York and openly planning to expand west toward the quarries, already cut carving labor on detailed work by up to 90 percent: a bust that took a hand carver one to two months is robot-milled in about two days and hand-finished in a few more. The robot does not replace the artisan, it multiplies one. A master models one capital, the machine roughs out the next hundred, and a carver finishes them by hand. A revival does not require returning to 1910 labor economics. It requires the design intent and a handful of trained people steering modern tools.

The Federal Tailwind

This is no longer a fringe position. On August 28, 2025, the President signed an executive order titled "Making Federal Architecture Beautiful Again," establishing classical and traditional architecture as the preferred style for federal buildings and the default in Washington, D.C. It followed a January 2025 memorandum directing the General Services Administration to deliver recommendations. Federal procurement is the single largest driver of the American building trades. When the federal government commits to classical stone, brick, and bronze, it pulls quarries, kilns, and foundries back into production at a scale no single city could justify on its own.

Seattle does not have to build this supply chain from scratch. It has to plug into one the federal government is already rebuilding, and add the regional pieces, Wilkeson stone, Renton clay, a Pacific Northwest terra cotta kiln, that make the buildings unmistakably ours. The logic is the same one that runs through this entire standard. Policy creates demand, demand justifies production, and production drops the cost. The materials to build a beautiful Seattle are not gone. They are waiting on a city willing to ask for them.

Against Vancouverization

Vancouver, BC went all-in on glass tower development in the 1990s and 2000s. The result is a skyline of identical glass condos that are already failing. Water infiltration. Facade delamination. Towers that look dated after 15 years and need re-cladding after 25. Vancouver is now dealing with billions of dollars in building envelope failures across its downtown core.

Seattle is making the same mistake. The South Lake Union and Denny Triangle building boom produced the same glass-and-panel towers, built to the same short lifespan, with the same energy inefficiency. Burnham Civic is organizing to make sure Seattle residents understand what "Vancouverization" actually looks like a generation later: ugly, expensive, and falling apart.

What TBC Is Doing

Burnham Civic is drafting a model material standard for Seattle based on the approved palette above and building the case for its adoption through the city's design review process. We are compiling a public database of Seattle buildings by material type, age, and assessed value to demonstrate the long-term economic case for durable construction. The Puget Standard color system is already specified in the 47 Shifts operational order and ready for deployment.

We are engaging the City of Charleston to learn how their Board of Architectural Review operates and how it can be adapted for Seattle. We are organizing public awareness of the Vancouverization problem so residents understand the real cost of the glass towers going up around them. And we are pushing for a material standard that means something: a city you can recognize from the air.
