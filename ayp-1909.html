The Year Seattle Built a City in a Forest

1909. The same year Burnham published the Plan of Chicago.

On the first day of June in 1909, a city of 237,000 people opened a world's fair on 250 acres of forested hillside overlooking Lake Washington, and over the next 138 days roughly 3.7 million visitors walked through the gates of the Alaska-Yukon-Pacific Exposition to see what a young Pacific city believed itself capable of becoming. They moved through white Beaux-Arts pavilions set among old-growth Douglas firs, looked down a grand central axis that pointed directly at the snowfield of Mount Rainier, and stood at the edge of cascading pools that dropped toward Portage Bay. The fair was deliberately positioned as an announcement to the rest of the country, and to the empires across the Pacific, that Seattle was no longer a gold rush camp but a permanent capital of the Northwest.

That same year, Daniel Burnham published the Plan of Chicago, and Seattle and Chicago were briefly working on the same scale and on the same question, asking what kind of city a 20th-century American metropolis ought to be. Chicago answered the question with a comprehensive plan that organized a century of investment, while Seattle answered it with a temporary exposition that lasted 138 days, and the difference between those two answers is most of the reason the two cities look the way they do today.

Opening Day, June 1, 1909

The mechanics of the opening ceremony were elaborate to the point of theater, and the historical record from HistoryLink and the Seattle Times makes clear that it was choreographed precisely because the organizers understood they had only one moment in which to introduce the fair to a national audience. Promptly at noon, J.E. Chilberg, the fair's president, stood in the natural amphitheater that had been carved out of the UW hillside and announced to a crowd estimated at close to 80,000 that he was about to notify President William Howard Taft, who at that hour stood in the East Room of the White House at the ready beside a small mahogany table.

On that table sat a telegraph key encrusted with gold nuggets from the placer claim of George Carmack, the prospector whose 1896 strike on Bonanza Creek had ignited the Klondike Gold Rush and, by extension, the Seattle that the fair was now celebrating. Taft pressed the key, the signal traveled across the country, and at the back of the platform in Seattle a large gong rang out. Cannons fired down at the lakefront. Behind the stage, John H. Leiter, a Civil War veteran and Washington Territory pioneer, pulled the ropes that released a gigantic American flag that had been close-furled between two large Douglas firs, and as the flag unfurled, a shower of confetti and thousands of small printed flags rained down over the crowd. The Klondike, in other words, was the gold that opened Seattle's front door, and the ceremony took pains to make that explicit.

What the AYP Was

The Alaska-Yukon-Pacific Exposition was organized to make a single argument: that Seattle was the natural gateway to Alaska, to the Yukon, and to the trans-Pacific economies of Japan, China, the Philippines, and Hawaii, and that the wealth which had been flowing through the city since 1897 ought to keep flowing through it permanently. The fair's themes ran in that order, with the Alaska Building and the Yukon Building anchoring the early entrance, the Manufactures and Agriculture Buildings filling the middle, and the trans-Pacific exhibits arranged along the lower terraces toward Lake Washington. Trade delegations from Japan, China, the Philippines, and Hawaii operated permanent pavilions that doubled as functioning consulates for the duration of the run, and the Pacific Rim identity that Seattle still carries was forged in those buildings during those four and a half months.

The site was laid out by John Charles Olmsted, the stepson of Frederick Law Olmsted and a partner in the Olmsted Brothers firm of Brookline, Massachusetts, the same office that had built Central Park, Boston's Emerald Necklace, and a 1903 parks plan for the City of Seattle. Olmsted designed the AYP grounds around the landscape rather than against it, preserving the existing stands of fir and cedar wherever the formal geometry would allow it, and he organized the entire 250-acre composition around a single sightline that he named the Rainier Vista. From the main court at the top of the hill, the eye traveled down a sequence of cascades, formal pools, and terraced gardens, past the Geyser Basin, and out toward the snowfield of Mount Rainier 60 miles to the southeast, an axis that survives today as the organizing spine of the University of Washington campus.

The pavilions themselves were Beaux-Arts in their idiom and provisional in their construction, built of wood and a plaster-fiber composite known as staff that allowed for elaborate classical ornament at temporary-building cost. The Agriculture Building, the Manufactures Building, the U.S. Government Building, and the Forestry Building each presented columns, domes, pediments, and friezes that read as permanent civic architecture, but were always understood by their builders to be intended for demolition at the close of the run, with the exception of a small number of structures that the University had agreed to retain.

The Photographer of the Fair

Almost everything we know visually about the AYP comes through the lens of one man, Frank H. Nowell, a photographer who had spent the previous decade in Alaska documenting the gold rush before settling in Seattle in 1903. Nowell was appointed official photographer of the Exposition by J.E. Chilberg, and over the course of the fair he produced more than 1,200 photographs that survive today in the Special Collections of the University of Washington Libraries, where they form the spine of the collection's AYP holdings. Because the buildings were torn down within months of the closing, Nowell's plates are functionally the only record we have of what the spaces actually felt like, and the colorized renderings produced for the Burnham Civic threads were built on top of his original glass-plate negatives. His work is supplemented by the photographs of the Oakes Photo Company and James Photo Company, both of which produced the postcard-format prints that filled the souvenir trade and that now circulate as the most widely reproduced AYP imagery.

Norway Day, Sweden, Hawaii, Suffrage

The AYP's calendar was structured around a sequence of "Special Days" that gave each major immigrant community, allied state, and constituency its own dedicated programming, and the most ambitious of these was Norway Day on August 30, 1909. The centerpiece of the day was the arrival, on the western shore of Lake Washington just below the Natural Amphitheater, of a 60-to-70-foot replica Norse longboat called The Viking, built specifically for the occasion by Sivert Sagstad, a Norwegian immigrant from Bergen who had settled in Ballard in 1905 and had founded the Ballard Boat Works on the north shore of Salmon Bay. The Viking sailed into the fairgrounds under the Norwegian flag and the American flag, was met at the quay by H.P. Rude and by Seattle Mayor John F. Miller, and was then led up the hill to the Stadium and on to the Amphitheater at the head of a parade of more than 500 marchers organized into nine tableaux representing successive periods of Norwegian history, from the Viking age through the early modern era.

Inside the Amphitheater, an audience of about 6,000 listened as a cablegram from King Haakon VII, sent that morning from Oslo, was read aloud, and the evening closed with a concert of the music of Edvard Grieg, the country's greatest 19th-century composer, who had died only two years earlier in Bergen. Earlier in the season, on July 31, the fair had hosted Swedish Day, which drew more than 40,000 visitors and ranked among the highest attended single days of the entire run, and the Scandinavian flavor of those two days reflected the demographic reality of Ballard and Magnolia in 1909, neighborhoods whose civic life still carries the imprint.

The fair's Hawaiian Day brought a different cultural import. The Hawaiian musician and ukulele virtuoso Ernest Kaai performed at the AYP with his ensemble, and contemporary accounts in the Pacific Coast press make clear that for many mainland visitors, this was the first time they had heard Hawaiian steel guitar and ukulele played live, an exposure that translated, within a few years, into a national craze for Hawaiian sheet music and recordings that would last well into the 1920s. A regional fair on the shore of Lake Washington thus turned out to be the launching point for a popular musical genre that crossed the country.

The AYP also hosted, that summer, the convention of the National American Woman Suffrage Association, and Suffrage Day at the fair brought thousands of organized suffragists onto the fairgrounds at a moment when the campaign for women's voting rights was approaching its critical decade. Washington women would, the following year, win full voting rights at the state level, a full decade before the ratification of the 19th Amendment in 1920, and the historical record consistently traces a line from the energy of the 1909 NAWSA convention at the AYP to the success of the 1910 Washington campaign.

The Boy and the Airship

Among the visitors that summer, according to a story repeated in HistoryLink and in every authoritative biography of the man, was a 28-year-old timber heir named William E. Boeing, a Yale-educated lumber entrepreneur then serving as president of the Greenwood Timber Company. Somewhere on the fairgrounds, almost certainly at the Geyser Basin or near the Lake Washington entrance, Boeing watched a small dirigible airship take flight, the same craft photographed by Oakes Photo Company drifting over the Court of Honor, piloted that summer by the aeronaut J.C. Mars and named "Alaska Yukon Pacific Exposition" for the duration of the run. It was the first time Boeing had seen a piloted flying machine, and the encounter set in motion a chain of events that, seven years later, in July of 1916, produced an aircraft company called Pacific Aero Products Co., on the Duwamish River in Seattle, an enterprise that was renamed Boeing Airplane Company in May of 1917 and that would, over the course of the following century, define the city's economy more thoroughly than any other private institution. The dirigible flight that started it all is preserved in Nowell's plates and in the Oakes postcards.

What Survived

When the fair closed on October 16, 1909, the demolition crews moved in quickly, and within a few seasons the great majority of the staff-and-wood pavilions had been pulled down or burned, but the parts of the fair that had been built into the underlying landscape continued to function as the campus of the University of Washington. The roads laid for the AYP became the campus's circulation system. The grading, the storm drainage, and the utility runs were absorbed into the university's permanent infrastructure. The Rainier Vista, which Olmsted had designed as the central axis of the fair, was retained as the central axis of the university and remains, more than a century later, the organizing spine of the campus, with Drumheller Fountain occupying the basin that had once held the cascading water feature called the Geyser.

One pavilion, the Fine Arts Building, was given to the university intact and remains standing today as Architecture Hall, a wood-framed structure with classical detailing that has continuously housed the architecture program for 117 years and is the only original AYP pavilion still in use. Beyond the immediate site, the work of the Olmsted Brothers firm in Seattle endured in the form of Volunteer Park, Frink Park, Colman Park, the Mount Baker Boulevard, and the broader park-and-boulevard system that the firm's 1903 plan had laid out and that the AYP commission helped continue through the late 1910s. Together, these are among the finest public landscapes in the city, and they constitute the durable physical inheritance of the 1903-to-1910 Olmsted era.

What Seattle Lost

What did not survive the AYP, however, was the civic ambition that had built it. The city of 1909 had demonstrated, beyond any reasonable doubt, that it possessed the institutional capacity to plan, finance, and execute a 250-acre international exposition, to attract nearly four million visitors, and to convert the whole effort, on its closing, into a permanent university campus, all within roughly four years from the first commission to the demolition crews. That is an extraordinary civic capability, and it was lost in the years that immediately followed.

The Olmsted parks plan continued to be implemented in fragments, but the comprehensive Bogue Plan of 1911, which would have given Seattle a civic center, a subway system, a planned waterfront, and a coherent street grid in the Burnham mold, was rejected by the voters in March of 1912, and after that defeat the city never again adopted a unified vision for its built environment. The contrast with Chicago, which absorbed Burnham's 1909 plan, taught it through Wacker's Manual to two generations of schoolchildren, and built progressively against it for the next 70 years, is the contrast that organizes the rest of the 20th century in both cities. The result, in Seattle, is the city we live in today, a city with a beautiful natural setting, with fragments of excellent planning preserved in the Olmsted parks and in the UW campus, and with vast stretches of incoherent and reactive development filling in the gaps in between, the consequence of having had the talent and the money but not the follow-through.

The 1909 Parallel

It is not coincidental that the AYP and the Plan of Chicago appeared in the same calendar year, because the late 1900s were the peak of the City Beautiful movement in America, and almost every major American city of that decade was commissioning comprehensive plans, hiring the leading architects and landscape architects of the era, and investing in monumental civic infrastructure on a scale that would not be matched again until after the Second World War. Washington, D.C. was rebuilding the National Mall around the McMillan Plan. Cleveland was constructing its Group Plan civic center, designed by the same firm that would design Cleveland Union Terminal a generation later. San Francisco had received Burnham's plan in 1905, and although the 1906 earthquake had derailed implementation, the conversation around classical civic form was active in every Pacific city. Seattle was, in short, an entirely typical participant in this national movement, and its AYP was an entirely typical City Beautiful project of the period.

The divergence came afterward. Where Chicago channeled the movement's energy into a durable plan backed by civic education, illustrated guidebooks, and serial implementation across decades, Seattle channeled the same energy into a temporary fair and, after the fair was demolished, did not commission, adopt, or build the comprehensive document that would have carried the lessons forward. The 1912 rejection of the Bogue Plan was the formal ratification of that divergence, and from 1912 onward Seattle's built environment was shaped by market actors, by individual developers, and by the limited imaginations of single-purpose agencies, rather than by a unifying civic instrument of the kind that Chicago possessed.

What TBC Is Doing

Burnham Civic takes its name from the movement that produced both the Plan of Chicago and the Alaska-Yukon-Pacific Exposition, and our basic position is that the 1909 moment in Seattle was not anomalous but illustrative, evidence of a civic capacity that still exists in the city's institutions, professions, and population, and that has lacked, since the 1912 rejection of the Bogue Plan, a vehicle through which to operate. The 47 Shifts is the vehicle we are building. It is the plan Seattle never wrote after the fair closed, structured as a comprehensive framework for the public infrastructure, material standards, and civic identity that the city has needed for a hundred and seventeen years and has not yet produced.

Returning the AYP to Public Memory

The first concrete piece of work, and the one this page is part of, is the recovery and circulation of the AYP itself as living public memory rather than as an obscure footnote in the university archives. Most people in Seattle today, including most lifelong residents, have never heard of the Alaska-Yukon-Pacific Exposition, and the imagery that defined the city's first international moment has been functionally invisible since the staff-and-wood pavilions came down in 1910. We are working to change that. Our archive program reaches back into the more than 1,200 surviving glass-plate negatives by Frank H. Nowell, the postcard-format photographs of the Oakes Photo Company and the James Photo Company, and the corresponding holdings at the National Nordic Museum, HistoryLink, and the Museum of History and Industry, in order to assemble a single, accessible, full-resolution visual record that the public can encounter without a research-library account.

That archive is paired with long-form publishing on this site and with a sustained narrative program on @TBCNewsAction, where each major AYP element, the Olmsted plan, the opening-day ceremony, the Sagstad longboat and Norway Day, the Special Days that ran through the summer, the Geyser Basin and the Cascades by night, the dirigible and the 28-year-old William Boeing watching it from the crowd, is given a thread of its own with restored photography and a citation trail back to the original sources. The point is not nostalgia. The point is that a city which has forgotten what it once built cannot easily imagine what it might build next, and the imagery is the operative instrument of civic memory, more than text and more than data.

Why World's Fairs Matter to Us

The dirigible-and-Boeing moment in 1909 is, on its surface, a charming anecdote, but it deserves to be taken seriously as a model of how cities actually generate the industries that define them for the next hundred years. Seattle's aerospace economy, which by the 2020s had grown to support more than 130,000 direct jobs in the region and had cumulatively produced the global commercial-aviation industry, can be traced, through a sequence of well-documented intermediate events, back to a 28-year-old timber executive standing on a fairground in his hometown and watching a piloted aircraft drift over the Court of Honor for the first time in his life. The 1962 Seattle World's Fair did the same thing on a different vector, producing the Space Needle, the Monorail, the Pacific Science Center, and the institutional and cultural infrastructure that, sixty years later, provided the substrate on which Microsoft and Amazon could be built.

The pattern is not unique to Seattle. The 1893 World's Columbian Exposition in Chicago seeded modern advertising, electrified urbanism, the public-museum movement, and the City Beautiful itself. The 1851 Great Exhibition in London produced the Crystal Palace, the Victoria and Albert Museum, and the museums quarter that organizes South Kensington to this day. The 1939 New York World's Fair produced the highway-and-television imagery that organized American consumer life for a generation. World's fairs, viewed at the right level of resolution, are technology-transfer and culture-transfer events compressed into a single physical place over a single short season, and their downstream effects, when the underlying city has the institutions to absorb them, run for sixty to a hundred years. Seattle's two fairs each produced a roughly century-long industrial inheritance, and that pattern is the operating assumption behind our planning for the 2062 centennial of the 1962 Fair.

The 2062 Centennial

The 2062 centennial of the 1962 Fair is thirty-six years away, which is closer than it sounds and which is exactly the lead time that the 1962 Fair itself required from its earliest planning conversations. Burnham Civic is working now on the political and design groundwork for that anniversary, in coordination with civic partners and members of our intelligence program. The specifics of that program, including the architectural and material standards we are developing for downtown Seattle, are reserved for members.

The Continuing Argument

The AYP proved that Seattle is capable of doing this work. The question that has hung over the city since the demolition crews left in 1910 is whether Seattle still wants to. Our reading of the city's institutions, neighborhoods, and constituencies is that the answer is yes, that the appetite for serious civic ambition is widely distributed and broadly unspoken, and that what has been missing is not the will but the platform on which the will can act. This page, this archive, this publishing program, the 47 Shifts framework, and the long lead-up to 2062 are the platform we are building.
